## Why service accounts

Because they are the favourite lateral-movement identity. Humans are monitored, alerted, trained. Service accounts log in quietly, from the same hosts, to the same shares, forever — until they do not.

## What the monitor does

- **Weekly rebuild** of the per-service-account behavioural profile across 90 days of telemetry:
  - Source hosts the account normally logs in from
  - SMB shares, cloud APIs, DB hosts it usually touches
  - Time-of-day distribution
- **Real-time detection** on the next day's telemetry: any touch outside the profile → analytic rule fires.

## Splunk rule skeleton

```text
index=windows sourcetype=WinEventLog:Security EventCode IN (4624,5140)
    Account_Name IN [| inputlookup svc_accounts.csv | fields account]
| eval svc=Account_Name, dest=coalesce(Computer, IP_Address)
| lookup svc_account_baseline.csv account AS svc OUTPUT expected_hosts
| where NOT match(dest, expected_hosts)
```

## What it has caught in the wild

- The phishing → credential-theft → lateral-movement intrusion walked through on `/triage`.
- A misconfigured CI worker logging in with the backup service account "just for a week".
- A legacy reporting job that was decommissioned on paper but still running from an orphaned host.

## Why it lives in the portfolio

Because the boring detection often matters more than the clever one. The Service-Account Baseline Monitor has caught more real intrusions for me than three of my favourite TTP-specific rules combined.
